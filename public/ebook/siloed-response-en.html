Executive SummaryKabatOne Industry Brief 2026

In Q1 2026, Axon acquired Carbyne — a next-generation 911 call-handling platform — for $625 million. Motorola Solutions has spent more than $3 billion acquiring public safety technology companies over the past decade. Hexagon absorbed Intergraph for $2.125 billion. These are not isolated bets. They are a structural signal: the era of fragmented public safety systems is ending.

For decades, governments managed emergencies through disconnected systems — video management, access control, computer-aided dispatch, and physical security information management all running in separate silos. Operators switched between screens, duplicated data, and lost critical seconds at the moments that mattered most.

Core Argument

The question is no longer whether to unify. It is how quickly each agency gets there — and on whose terms. Agencies that define their technology strategy now will choose a platform that fits their environment. Agencies that wait will find their choices shaped by consolidation decisions of global vendors whose primary design context is not LATAM.

KabatOne was designed natively for this moment — not assembled through acquisition. This document explains why the shift is happening, what it requires, and how agencies can build the business case for unified response.

Motorola Solutions had spent the previous decade systematically acquiring the components of a unified public safety stack: Spillman Technologies (CAD, $221M), Avigilon (video, ~$1B), Rave Mobile Safety (mass notification, $553M), RapidDeploy (cloud CAD, $241M). These acquisitions share a common thesis: the future of public safety is not point solutions. It is integrated operational environments where video, dispatch, GIS, AI, and field response work as one.

Category Shift

The vendors who arrived at this conclusion spent billions to build what the market is demanding. KabatOne was built this way from the start. This is not a product race. It is a category shift. For government agencies evaluating their infrastructure today — in Mexico, Peru, across Latin America, and in the United States — the strategic question is not whether to unify, but when, and how.

CH 01 — Why Systems Were Always SiloedKabatOne Industry Brief 2026

The answer is not poor planning. It is the natural consequence of how the technology evolved. Each category emerged from a different engineering lineage, in a different decade, optimized for a different function.

Figure 1 — The Four Technology Lineages (1970–2020)
1970s–1990sCADCall intake, unit dispatch, incident logging. Built for telephone + radio. No GIS or video integration.
1980s–2000sAccessBadging, perimeter, entry logs. Facilities management problem — not public safety operations.
1990s–2000sVMSVideo capture, storage, playback. Optimized for codec efficiency — not dispatch or GIS integration.
2000s–2010sPSIMAggregation layer for the other three. Surfaces information — does not coordinate response.
Each lineage remains a separate track through 2020 — disconnected by design, not by accident

Video Management (VMS) — 1990s–2000s

Early VMS vendors — Milestone, Genetec, Avigilon — built software optimized for video ingestion, recording, and playback. They were not designed to share data with dispatch systems or GIS layers. Video was a recording tool, not an operational layer.

Computer-Aided Dispatch (CAD) — 1970s–1990s

CAD is the operational backbone of emergency response, built around telephone-based call intake and radio dispatch. The core data model — incident type, unit ID, location, timestamp — was designed for a world without IP cameras, GPS tracking, or real-time GIS overlay.

Access Control & PSIM

Access control emerged from building security and facilities management — rarely a public safety operations priority. PSIM emerged as an aggregation layer for the other three, but it surfaces information rather than coordinating response. PSIM deployments were notoriously expensive, brittle, and slow to adapt.

The result: an operational environment built from independent tracks. Each category had its own vendors, data formats, operator interfaces, and procurement cycle. They were never designed to work together — and in most agencies today, they still do not.

The Dispatch Gap

According to NENA data, the average time from a 911 call to unit dispatch is between 90 and 150 seconds in a typical U.S. urban center. A significant portion is spent on data entry — not decision-making. APCO has documented that telecommunicators managing four or more simultaneous screens experience measurably higher error rates and longer call-to-dispatch intervals than those operating from a unified interface.

The Handoff Problem

When a camera feed shows a developing incident, the operator must: identify it on the VMS, locate the address on a separate GIS tool, open the CAD system, create a new incident record, manually enter location and incident type, assign a unit, and initiate radio contact. Each handoff is a friction point. Each friction point introduces delay measured in outcomes — not seconds.

The Audit and Compliance Cost

After an incident, reconstructing a timeline from four separate systems — each with its own export format — is time-consuming, error-prone, and legally problematic. NG911 Phase II mandates and NIEM-based data sharing requirements assume agencies can produce integrated, timestamped records of incident lifecycle. Fragmented architectures make this significantly harder to achieve.

1. Video Ingestion at Scale

Connect cameras from any manufacturer via ONVIF — no proprietary VMS license per ecosystem. Handle live feeds, recorded footage, AI-generated alerts, and mobile video from field devices in a single interface. Video that must be toggled to manually is not integrated — it is adjacent.

2. Real-Time GIS Overlay

Every incident, unit, camera, and sensor must be geolocated and displayed on a common operational map. The GIS layer is not a reporting tool — it is the operational canvas on which command decisions are made. Dispatching a unit without knowing where it is relative to the incident and other units is a coordination failure by design.

3. CAD Connectivity and NG911 Compliance

A platform that shows video and GIS but cannot initiate or receive CAD workflow is not a unified response environment — it is a monitoring environment. NG911 compliance (NENA i3) ensures participation in next-generation emergency communication networks including text-to-911, video-to-911, and multi-agency data sharing.

4. AI Analytics Embedded in Workflow

Object detection, crowd density analysis, license plate recognition — these are only useful if they surface actionable alerts within the command workflow, attached to the relevant GIS location and video feed, with the ability to initiate dispatch. AI that outputs to a separate alert dashboard is one more screen to monitor.

5. Open Architecture — Integration, Not Replacement

A genuine unified platform connects to existing cameras via ONVIF, integrates with existing CAD via standard APIs, and provides a migration path rather than a migration mandate. A platform that demands rip-and-replace is not a platform — it is a vendor lock-in strategy.

CH 06 — Diagnosing Your Current ArchitectureKabatOne Industry Brief 2026

Answer these honestly before any procurement conversation. Each "no" or "not easily" is a quantifiable operational liability.

  • 01How many separate interfaces does an operator manage during a critical incident? If three or more, each is a coordination gap.
  • 02What is your current call-to-dispatch time, and how much of it is data entry — not decision-making?
  • 03When a camera alert occurs, how many manual steps does it take to create a CAD record? Each step is a delay that compounds under volume.
  • 04Can your agency produce a complete timestamped incident lifecycle record from four separate systems in under 30 minutes? If not, you have an audit liability.
  • 05Are your video, GIS, and dispatch systems from different vendors with separate contracts, maintenance cycles, and operator training programs?
  • 06How long does it take to onboard a new operator to full proficiency across all systems? Fragmented environments have measurably longer ramp times.
  • 07Does your current architecture support NG911 Phase II requirements — text, video, and data alongside voice call intake?
  • 08Can you share structured incident data with partner agencies in NIEM-compatible formats without a manual export process?
Interpretation

If three or more of these answers are "no" or "not easily," fragmentation is your operational liability — not a future risk. It is a current cost distributed across training, overtime, integration maintenance, and incident reconstruction. The business case for unification starts here.

The True Cost of Fragmentation

The business case for unification is often framed around the cost of the new platform. The more accurate frame is the total cost of the status quo: multiple vendor contracts with annual maintenance fees, multiple operator training programs, custom integration development, incident reconstruction costs, compliance remediation, and IT overhead. When aggregated over a five-year horizon, the economic case for unification is typically compelling. Fragmentation costs are invisible as a category — even when they are large in aggregate.

CH 10 — Vendor Evaluation QuestionsKabatOne Industry Brief 2026

Use these questions in any vendor evaluation session. The answers will reveal whether the platform is genuinely unified — or a better-designed aggregation layer.

  • 01Camera integration: Does the platform connect to cameras from any manufacturer via ONVIF — without requiring proprietary hardware or an additional VMS license per ecosystem?
  • 02CAD integration: Is the dispatch module integrated natively, or does the platform require a separate third-party CAD system to function?
  • 03GIS capability: Is the GIS layer operational — dispatch-ready, with real-time unit positioning — or only informational (map display without workflow integration)?
  • 04Implementation timeline: What is your documented go-live timeline for a command center of our scale? Can you provide references from comparable deployments?
  • 05Multi-agency experience: Have you deployed in environments where multiple agencies share a single common operating picture?
  • 06NG911 compliance: How does the platform handle Phase II — text-to-911, video-to-911, and structured data alongside voice call intake?
  • 07Data standards: What is the data export format? Does it support NIEM for multi-agency and federal data sharing?
  • 08Existing infrastructure: Can we connect our existing cameras, radios, and legacy systems — or does integration require replacing equipment?
  • 09Post-go-live support: What does your ongoing support model look like for government clients — SLA, escalation path, local presence?
  • 10Data portability: If we transition to a different platform in the future, how do we export our incident and operational data?

Conclusion

The Agencies That Act Now

The convergence happening in the public safety technology market is not speculative. It is documented in acquisition records, standards bodies, market data, and operational deployments across Latin America and North America.

The vendors that will dominate the category in five years are assembling the unified stack today — through M&A, through standards adoption, and through deployment experience that only comes from operating at scale in complex environments.

For government agencies, the strategic question is not whether unified response platforms will become the operational standard. They already are. The question is how quickly each agency gets there, and on whose terms.

Agencies that define their technology strategy now will have the advantage of choosing a platform that fits their environment, their existing systems, and their operational requirements. Agencies that wait will face a market where their choices are increasingly shaped by global vendors whose primary design context is not LATAM — and whose integration models assume replacement, not connection.

The industry is converging. The standards are in place. The deployments are proven.
